Destinations

Orthodox churches organized by region — with architectural notes, history, and some personal thoughts on each one. This list is always growing.

 My Home Parish

Before I get into all the grand cathedrals and UNESCO sites — this is where it started for me. Saints Constantine and Helen Greek Orthodox Church. It's not the biggest church. It doesn't have gold-plated domes visible from a kilometre away. But this is the place where my faith actually took root, and I don't think any church in the world will ever mean more to me than this one.

There's something I always say about Orthodox churches, and this one is a perfect example of it: even when the exterior is humble, you walk inside and you are completely stopped in your tracks. The iconostasis, the icons, the candles, the smell of incense — every Orthodox church in the world, no matter how small, carries that same spirit inside. It's one of the things I love most about this tradition. Beauty is not optional. It's theologically required.

Saints Constantine and Helen Greek Orthodox Church

British Columbia, Canada

This is my home parish. Named after Saint Constantine the Great — the Roman Emperor who legalized Christianity in 313 AD — and his mother Saint Helen, who is credited with finding the True Cross in Jerusalem. Constantine and Helen are among the most important saints in Orthodox Christianity; they're sometimes called "Equal-to-the-Apostles" for their role in allowing the faith to spread openly. There's a kind of poetry in the fact that my journey to Orthodoxy began in a church named after the man who changed the legal status of Christianity forever.

Canada

Canada has a surprisingly rich Orthodox presence, brought over by waves of immigration from Greece, Ukraine, Russia, Serbia, Romania, and the Middle East throughout the 19th and 20th centuries. Most people don't realize how many Orthodox parishes are tucked into Canadian cities. I'm just beginning to explore these.

Saint Nicholas Russian Orthodox Cathedral

Victoria, BC

One of the most beautiful Orthodox churches in British Columbia, sitting quietly in Victoria. Built in the early 20th century by Russian immigrants, it has the classic onion dome architecture that immediately tells you what tradition it belongs to. I've been meaning to visit for ages — it's only a ferry ride away.

Saint Sophia Ukrainian Orthodox Cathedral

Winnipeg, Manitoba

Named after the Hagia Sophia in Constantinople, this Winnipeg cathedral is a landmark of Ukrainian-Canadian identity and faith. Manitoba has one of the largest Ukrainian communities outside of Ukraine itself, and Orthodox Christianity is a central part of that heritage. The building is absolutely stunning — grand and elaborate in the way Ukrainian church architecture tends to be.

Saint George Antiochian Orthodox Cathedral

Montréal, Québec

The Antiochian Orthodox tradition in North America is something a lot of people don't know about — it's the church that traces its roots directly to Antioch (in modern Syria), one of the great early Christian cities. This Montreal cathedral has been a spiritual home to Arabic-speaking Orthodox Christians in Canada since the early 1900s.

United States

Orthodoxy in America is undergoing a bit of a quiet renaissance right now, with a notable number of converts joining alongside the traditional immigrant communities. There are incredible churches scattered across the country — some grand, some small and old and perfect in their own way.

Holy Trinity Russian Orthodox Cathedral

Chicago, Illinois

Designed in 1903 by none other than Louis Sullivan — one of the most important architects in American history and the man who mentored Frank Lloyd Wright. The building is a fascinating hybrid: Russian Orthodox in its spiritual purpose, but unmistakably Sullivan in its ornamental detailing. A genuinely unique building in American architectural history.

Cathedral of the Holy Virgin Protection

New York City, New York

One of New York's hidden gems. This Russian Orthodox cathedral in lower Manhattan has been a spiritual landmark for the city's Orthodox community since the 19th century. Walking into it is like stepping into another world — the contrast with the surrounding streets of New York City is pretty striking, and that contrast is kind of the point.

Saint Anthony Greek Orthodox Monastery

Florence, Arizona

Founded in 1995 by monks from the Holy Mountain of Athos in Greece, this monastery in the Arizona desert has become one of the most significant Orthodox monasteries in North America. Thousands of pilgrims visit every year. The contrast of Byzantine iconography and golden mosaics set against the Sonoran desert landscape is genuinely something else.

Saint Sava Serbian Orthodox Cathedral

Chicago, Illinois

The oldest Serbian Orthodox cathedral in North America, consecrated in 1914. Named after Saint Sava — the 13th century Serbian monk and bishop who is considered the patron saint of the Serbian people. The cathedral has served as the spiritual and cultural center of Chicago's Serbian community for over a century.

Annunciation Greek Orthodox Cathedral

Baltimore, Maryland

Designed by the legendary architect Mies van der Rohe, which makes it one of the most architecturally significant Orthodox churches in America. It's a fascinating case of modernist architecture being used for ancient liturgical purposes — Mies designed it in the 1950s, and the result is a church that somehow manages to feel both thoroughly modern and genuinely sacred at the same time.

Greece

Greece is where so much of this tradition was shaped — the language of the New Testament, the early Church Fathers, the Ecumenical Councils. The landscape is covered in Orthodox churches, from tiny whitewashed chapels on cliff edges to ancient monasteries perched on dramatic hillsides.

Church of the Assumption, Oia

Santorini, Greece

The iconic blue-domed churches of Santorini are probably the most photographed Orthodox buildings in the world. The Church of the Assumption in Oia is among the most beautiful — perched on the volcanic caldera with that deep Aegean blue dome against the white walls and the blue of the sea. It's almost too beautiful to be real, which feels very appropriate for an Orthodox church.

Osios Loukas Monastery

Distomo, Central Greece

A UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the finest examples of Byzantine architecture surviving today. Built in the 10th and 11th centuries, the monastery's interior contains extraordinary Byzantine mosaics that have barely been touched since they were created a thousand years ago. The level of preservation is astonishing. It's like stepping directly into the Byzantine Empire.

Panagia Ekatontapiliani

Paros Island, Greece

One of the oldest Christian churches in the world still in active liturgical use, traditionally dated to the 4th century. The name means "Our Lady of a Hundred Doors" — legend says that 99 doors have been found, and the 100th will be discovered when Constantinople is returned to the Greeks. It's a pilgrimage site and an active parish simultaneously, which says something about the continuity of Orthodox life.

Russia

After the Baptism of Rus in 988 AD, Russia became the largest Orthodox nation in the world — and it shows. The sheer density and grandeur of Russian Orthodox architecture is unlike anything else. Saint Petersburg alone has more extraordinary churches than most countries. Here are some that I keep returning to in my research:

Church of the Savior on Spilled Blood

Saint Petersburg, Russia

Built 1883–1907 on the exact spot where Tsar Alexander II was assassinated by a grenade attack in 1881. His son Alexander III insisted the memorial church be built right there, over the bloodstained cobblestones — those original cobblestones are still visible inside. The exterior is incredible, but the interior is what really gets you: over 7,000 square metres of mosaics covering every wall, pillar, and vaulted surface. Under Soviet rule it served as a potato warehouse. It reopened as a museum in 1997 and has never been used for actual services. Still one of the most visited churches in Russia.

Saint Isaac's Cathedral

Saint Petersburg, Russia

The largest Orthodox cathedral in Saint Petersburg and the fourth largest domed cathedral in the world. It took 40 years to build (1818–1858), and the numbers behind it are staggering: 112 granite columns, 43 types of decorative stone and semi-precious minerals in the interior, a dome that required the use of a newly invented steam engine to install. The golden dome is visible from all over the city. During the Soviet era it was converted into a museum of atheism — a somewhat ironic use for such an overwhelming statement of religious architecture.

Kazan Cathedral

Saint Petersburg, Russia

Designed by Andrei Voronikhin (1801–1811) and directly inspired by Saint Peter's Basilica in Rome — a conscious statement that Orthodox Russia was the equal of Catholic Europe. The defining feature is its massive curved colonnade of 96 Corinthian columns sweeping along Nevsky Prospect. Inside, it houses the revered Kazan Icon of the Mother of God, one of the most important icons in Russian Orthodoxy. The cathedral was actually turned into a museum of the history of religion and atheism during the Soviet era — they literally put anti-religious exhibits inside one of Russia's holiest churches.

Trinity Cathedral

Alexander Nevsky Monastery, Saint Petersburg

The centrepiece of the Alexander Nevsky Monastery — the most important Orthodox monastery in Saint Petersburg. Built in the Baroque style in the 18th century, the cathedral contains the relics of Saint Alexander Nevsky, the 13th century Russian prince and military hero who defended Russia from Swedish and Teutonic invasions and was later canonized. Peter the Great personally commissioned the transfer of his relics to this monastery in 1724, travelling part of the way in the boat that carried the relics himself.

Smolny Convent

Saint Petersburg, Russia

One of the most elegant Baroque buildings in Russia, designed by Bartolomeo Francesco Rastrelli — the same architect behind the Winter Palace — for Empress Elizabeth in 1748. The blue and white facade with golden domes is absolutely beautiful, and the proportions are perfect in a way that photographs don't quite capture. Fun (somewhat sad) fact: Rastrelli's original design included an enormous 140-metre bell tower that would have been the tallest in Russia. It was never built because Elizabeth ran out of money. You can still see where it was supposed to go.

Chesme Church

Saint Petersburg, Russia

Completely unlike anything else in Russian Orthodox architecture. Built between 1777 and 1780 to commemorate Russia's decisive naval victory over the Ottoman fleet at the Battle of Chesme (1770), this church looks more like something from a Gothic fairytale than a traditional Orthodox building. The white exterior with intricate pink Gothic tracery and pinnacles was designed by the architect Yury Felten. It was completely deconsecrated under Soviet rule and converted into a branch of the Naval Museum, which at least probably preserved it from further damage.

Kronstadt Naval Cathedral

Kronstadt, Russia

Built between 1903 and 1913 specifically for the sailors and officers of the Russian Imperial Navy, this cathedral on Kotlin Island was directly inspired by the Hagia Sophia. The dome is 26 metres in diameter and rises 52 metres above the ground — it served as a navigational landmark for ships entering Saint Petersburg for decades. The interior walls were originally lined with bronze plaques listing the names of every Russian naval officer who died in service. The Soviets stripped everything out and turned it into a cinema, then a concert hall. A major restoration completed in 2013 returned it to a working church.

Assumption Cathedral of Smolensk

Smolensk, Russia

Perched dramatically on a hill above the Dnieper River, this cathedral has stood as the spiritual and visual centrepiece of Smolensk for centuries. The original 12th century church was destroyed by Polish forces in 1611; the current building was constructed in the late 17th and early 18th centuries in the Ukrainian Baroque style. Smolensk itself has one of the saddest and most dramatic histories of any Russian city — it was devastated by Napoleon in 1812 and almost completely destroyed again during World War II. The cathedral survived both and still stands.

Kizhi Pogost

Kizhi Island, Lake Onega, Russia

UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the greatest feats of wooden architecture in human history. The Church of the Transfiguration (1714) has 22 domes arranged in a spectacular ascending cascade — all carved from Karelian aspen wood, all built without iron nails (or, in some accounts, with an absolute minimum of them). The legend goes that the master carpenter built it with a single axe and then threw the axe into the lake saying "there was not and will not be another like it." Standing on that remote island in Lake Onega, surrounded by water and sky, it genuinely looks like it grew out of the landscape.

Church of St. Igor of Chernigov

Peredellno, Moscow, Russia

A more recent church — consecrated in 2014 — but one that shows how the Russian Orthodox architectural tradition is very much alive and being carried forward. Named after Prince Igor of Chernigov, a 12th century prince who was martyred and later canonized, the church is built in the Russian Revival style with the multicoloured onion domes, tent roofs, and ornate facade details that define that tradition. The interior is covered in hand-painted frescoes and mosaics that took years to complete.

Church of the Intercession at Fili

Fili, Moscow, Russia

Built between 1690 and 1693 for Lev Naryshkin — the uncle of Peter the Great — this church is considered one of the masterpieces of the "Naryshkin Baroque" style, a uniquely Russian blend of traditional Orthodox architecture with Western Baroque decoration. The red brick walls with ornate white limestone details and the distinctive tiered octagonal tower rising above the refectory make it immediately recognizable. It's one of the best-preserved examples of pre-Petrine Russian architecture, and it survived both the Napoleonic occupation of Moscow in 1812 and the Soviet era intact.

Eastern Europe

Outside of Russia, Eastern Europe has an incredible density of Orthodox architectural heritage — from the ancient monasteries of Serbia to the great national cathedrals of Bulgaria, Estonia, Ukraine, and Georgia. Orthodoxy is woven into the cultural and national identity of these countries in ways that go very deep.

Cathedral of Saint Sava

Belgrade, Serbia

One of the largest Orthodox churches in the world — the dome is 40 metres wide and the interior can hold 10,000 people. It sits on the Vrčar Plateau, the exact spot where in 1595 the Ottoman Grand Vizier Koca Sinan Pasha burned the relics of Saint Sava — the 13th century monk and first Archbishop of Serbia who is venerated as the patron saint of the Serbian people. Construction began in 1935, was interrupted by two world wars and communist rule, and the main structure wasn't completed until 2004. The gold-leaf mosaics inside are still being completed.

Alexander Nevsky Cathedral

Sofia, Bulgaria

One of the largest Orthodox cathedrals in the world, with seating for 5,000 people and standing room for 10,000. Built between 1882 and 1912 to commemorate the 200,000 Russian soldiers who died in the Russo-Turkish War of 1877–78, which liberated Bulgaria from almost five centuries of Ottoman rule. Named after Alexander Nevsky, the patron saint of Tsar Alexander II of Russia. The central dome is covered in real gold leaf. The whole thing is a statement of gratitude — Bulgaria to Russia — written in stone and gold.

Alexander Nevsky Cathedral, Tallinn

Tallinn, Estonia

This cathedral has an interesting history because it's never been just a religious building — it's always been political. Built between 1894 and 1900 during the period of Russian Imperial rule over Estonia, it was positioned on Toompea Hill directly opposite the Estonian parliament building. After Estonian independence, there were serious discussions about demolishing it. It survived, and it remains the largest Orthodox church in Estonia. Whether you see it as a beautiful building or a symbol of foreign domination depends entirely on your perspective — and that ambiguity is part of what makes it fascinating.

Annunciation Cathedral of Kharkiv

Kharkiv, Ukraine

The tallest Orthodox church in Ukraine, with a bell tower reaching 89.5 metres — making it the tallest structure in Kharkiv when it was built between 1888 and 1901. The Byzantine Eclectic style blends traditional Orthodox architectural forms with the more decorative sensibilities of late 19th century European architecture. The cathedral is currently in a war zone — Kharkiv is the second largest city in Ukraine and has been the target of repeated Russian attacks since 2022 — which makes every image of it take on a different emotional weight for me.

Holy Trinity Cathedral (Sameba)

Tbilisi, Georgia

Georgia adopted Christianity in 327 AD — making it one of the first countries in the world to do so — and the Georgian Orthodox Church is one of the oldest and most distinctive in the Orthodox world. Sameba Cathedral, built between 1995 and 2004, is the largest cathedral in Georgia and the third tallest Orthodox church in the world (main tower: 87 metres). It draws from medieval Georgian church architecture rather than the Byzantine or Russian traditions, which gives it a completely different visual vocabulary. The interior is covered in traditional Georgian frescoes.

Africa

This is one of the things that surprised me most when I started exploring Orthodoxy — the ancient and deeply rooted presence of Christianity in Africa. The Ethiopian Orthodox Church and the Coptic Orthodox Church of Egypt are among the oldest Christian communities in the world, predating most of what we think of as "European" Christianity by centuries.

Church of Our Lady Mary of Zion

Axum, Ethiopia

This church in ancient Axum is one of the most sacred in all of Ethiopia — and in all of African Christianity. It is said to house the Ark of the Covenant, brought to Ethiopia by Menelik I, the son of Solomon and the Queen of Sheba. Regardless of what you believe about that claim, the site has been a place of Christian pilgrimage since the 4th century. The current church was built in the 1950s, but the tradition it continues is ancient beyond measure.

Saint George Cathedral

Addis Ababa, Ethiopia

Built in 1896 to commemorate Ethiopia's victory over Italian forces at the Battle of Adwa — one of the most significant events in African history, being the first major military defeat of a European colonial power by an African nation. Emperor Menelik II had the cathedral built as a thanksgiving to Saint George, patron saint of Ethiopia. Both Menelik II and Emperor Haile Selassie were crowned here. The octagonal structure blends traditional Ethiopian and European architectural influences.

The Hanging Church (El Moallaqa)

Coptic Cairo, Egypt

One of the oldest churches in Egypt, built over the gatehouse of the Babylon fortress in what is now the Old Cairo neighbourhood. The name comes from the fact that it appears to hang in the air above the old Roman towers below. The Coptic Orthodox Church is one of the most ancient branches of Christianity — founded by Saint Mark the Evangelist in the 1st century AD. This church has been a continuous place of Christian worship for somewhere between 1,600 and 1,900 years.

Debre Birhan Selassie

Gondar, Ethiopia

Look up the next time you're in this church. The entire ceiling is covered with 135 painted cherub faces staring down at you — row after row of them, large eyes and round cheeks, all watching. It's one of the most striking interior decorations in any church anywhere in the world. The building itself dates to the late 17th century and is one of the best-preserved examples of Ethiopian ecclesiastical architecture, which is completely distinct from Byzantine or Russian traditions — it developed in isolation and follows its own logic entirely.

Cathedral of Saint Nicholas

Johannesburg, South Africa

The seat of the Greek Orthodox Metropolis of South Africa, serving the substantial Greek immigrant community and their descendants in South Africa. The Greek Orthodox presence in southern Africa dates back over a century, and this cathedral is one of the most prominent Orthodox buildings on the continent. It's a reminder that Orthodoxy isn't just a religion of Eastern Europe and the Middle East — it's genuinely a world religion with communities on every inhabited continent.

Japan

This one always surprises people. Orthodox Christianity in Japan? It's real, and it has been for over 150 years — and it produced one of the most unexpected and beautiful churches I've ever come across.

Nikolai-dō (Holy Resurrection Cathedral)

Tokyo, Japan

Standing in Ochanomizu, Tokyo, is a Byzantine domed cathedral that looks completely incongruous with everything around it — in the best possible way. Built between 1884 and 1891, it was designed by the Russian architect Mikhail Shchurупов and completed under the supervision of Josiah Conder, the British architect who brought Western-style architecture to Meiji-era Japan. It's named after Saint Nicholas of Japan — the Russian Orthodox missionary Nikolai Kasatkin who spent over 50 years in Japan converting thousands of Japanese people to Orthodoxy. He's now venerated as a saint by both the Russian and Japanese Orthodox churches. The building was badly damaged in the 1923 Great Kantō Earthquake and rebuilt. It's still an active parish today.
